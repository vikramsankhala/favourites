📐 Audio Theory & Best Practices

Signal Flow Basics

Audio signal flow traces sound from source to output. The signal begins as analog voltage representing sound waves, travels through cables to preamps and mixers, then to amplifiers and speakers. Each component acts as a gain stage — understanding this flow is critical for troubleshooting and optimizing your setup.

Signal Flow

Balanced vs Unbalanced

Unbalanced (1/4" TS, RCA): One active wire + ground. Adequate for short runs (<15 ft) but develops hum over longer distances. Use for guitar, pedals, and short instrument runs.

Balanced (XLR, TRS): Two signal wires + ground. Rejects common-mode interference via CMR. Can run 100+ feet without noise. Use for mics, PA runs, and long cable runs.

Cable Types

Gain Staging

Keep levels at -12 to -14 dBFS in your DAW to prevent clipping while preserving headroom. On the mixer: set each channel so meters peak in the green (not red). Phantom power (48V) is required for condenser mics — enable only on channels with condenser mics.

Gain Staging

Impedance & Power

PA amplifiers deliver max power at specific impedance (commonly 4Ω). Active speakers have built-in amps — connect XLR from mixer main out. Keep power cables separate from audio cables to reduce electromagnetic interference by ~40%.

🎼 Music & Lyrics Composition & Sheet Music

Lyrics Writing

  • Structure — Verse, chorus, bridge; AABA or verse-chorus-verse
  • Rhyme schemes — AABB, ABAB, ABCB; internal rhymes for flow
  • Theme & imagery — One clear idea per song; show, don't tell
  • Melody-first vs lyrics-first — Hum a tune and fit words, or write words and find melody
Songwriting

Music Composition

  • Chord progressions — I–IV–V, I–V–vi–IV; use your keyboard to experiment
  • Melody — Stay within vocal range; use Ableton's piano roll to sketch
  • Arrangement — Intro, verse, pre-chorus, chorus, bridge, outro
  • Dynamics — Build and release; sparse vs full sections
Composition in DAW

Sheet Music & Notation

🌏 Fusion — Indian Classical, Western Jams & Global Percussion

Where raga meets riff, where taal meets groove — let the melody breathe, let the rhythm wander. Spontaneous, flowing, alive.

Indian Classical — Vocal & Instrumental

Weave Hindustani and Carnatic traditions into your jams. The voice becomes an instrument; the instrument sings. Sur (pitch) and laya (tempo) hold everything together.

  • Ragas — Bhairav, Yaman, Malkauns, Darbari; mix morning and evening ragas for mood
  • Sargam — Sa Re Ga Ma Pa Dha Ni; vocalise and improvise on the scale
  • Alaap — Unmetered exploration; let the raga unfold slowly
  • Taans — Fast melodic runs; build intensity, then release
A Heer FusionRaag BageshreeBrooklyn Raga Massive

Embellishments & Improvisation

Ornamentation gives life to the note. A single swar can bend, slide, quiver — meend, gamak, murkhi, andolan. On guitar: bends, vibrato, slides. On keys: sustain, grace notes.

  • Meend — Glide between notes; smooth, vocal-like
  • Gamak — Oscillation, shake; adds weight and emotion
  • Murkhi — Quick ornamental flourishes
  • Andolan — Gentle sway; microtonal movement
  • Let phrases breathe — space is part of the melody
Raag Sarang FusionStudio Improv

Western Jams — Blues, Jazz, Rock

Blues scales over a drone. Jazz chords under a raga phrase. Rock energy with tabla bols. The jam room is where borders dissolve.

  • Blues — Minor pentatonic + blue notes; call-and-response with vocal alaap
  • Jazz — ii–V–I meets raga vadi–samvadi; extended harmonies
  • Rock — Power chords + driving rhythm; layer with taans or sargam
  • Improvise in rounds — one voice leads, others respond
Raga Jazz RockGuitar Improv JamLofi Jazz Jam

Global Percussion

Tabla, djembe, cajón, darbuka, congas, frame drums — the world's heartbeat. Your electronic drum kit can trigger samples; add loops from Ableton. Layer taal cycles with 4/4, 6/8, 12/8.

  • Tabla — Teentaal (16), Keherwa (8), Dadra (6); bols: dha, dhin, ta, tin
  • Djembe — West African; bass, tone, slap
  • Cajón — Peruvian; punchy, sits under acoustic jams
  • Frame drum / Daf — Persian; circular, meditative

Urdu & Semi-Persian Lyrics — Spontaneous & Flowing

Let words drift like ghazal — each couplet a breath, each line a thread. Urdu and Persian lend themselves to fluid, image-rich verse. No rigid rhyme; let the sound carry the sense.

Style & Flow

  • Ghazal — Radif (repeated word), qafiya (rhyme); couplets stand alone
  • Free verse — No strict metre; follow the melody
  • Imagery — Pani, chand, raat, dil, yaad — water, moon, night, heart, memory
  • Breath — Short phrases, long phrases; let the line dictate

Jam Room Fusion Flow

Start with a drone or a simple chord. One person lays a raga phrase or a blues lick. Another answers with percussion or a counter-melody. Add vocals when the mood rises. Let the structure emerge — don't force it. Record everything; the best moments are often unplanned.
