Three thousand years ago, in the forests and royal courts of the Gangetic plain, thinkers began dismantling every comfortable assumption about identity, reality, and death. What they found — or what found them — would become the bedrock of Hindu philosophy, shape Buddhism and Jainism, astonish Schopenhauer, electrify Thoreau, ignite the Indian independence movement, and now pulse quietly inside debates about consciousness, AI sentience, climate ethics, and the search for meaning in a secular age.

This curriculum moves through twelve progressively deepening modules, from the archaeological world of the Vedas to the subtlest reaches of Advaita metaphysics — always keeping one eye on the contemporary horizon.

Foundations · History · Archaeology

Origins — The World of the Vedas

5 lessons · 72 min

Picture the Indus Valley, circa 1500 BCE: pastoral settlements on the banks of the Saraswati and Gangā, where wandering poet-priests — the ṛṣis — composed hymns to fire, sky, and soma in a language so precise it still governs Sanskrit grammar. They did not claim to invent these hymns; they said they heard them. The Vedas are śruti — "that which is heard" — as if the cosmos itself were dictating to an attentive ear. The Rigveda's famous Nasadiya Sukta (Creation Hymn) ends not with an answer but with a question: "Who really knows? Who will here proclaim it? Whence was it produced? … Perhaps it formed itself, or perhaps it did not — the one who looks down on it, in the highest heaven, only he knows — or perhaps he does not know."

This opening module places you inside that living oral culture — the yajña (ritual fire sacrifice), the gurukula (forest learning community), the tension between Vedic orthodoxy and the radical questionings that would produce the Upanishads. Archaeologically, we draw on recent findings from the Rakhigarhi DNA study (2019) and the ongoing debates about the Aryan Migration Theory to understand who these people actually were.

↳ Contemporary resonance: The search for origins parallels today's ancient DNA genomics (David Reich's Who We Are and How We Got Here, 2018) and the political battles over India's Hindutva historiography. The oral tradition speaks directly to debates about embodied cognition and non-textual knowledge in education theory.

Brahman — The Ground of All Being

6 lessons · 90 min

The Taittirīya Upanishad describes a young student, Bhṛgu, returning to his father Varuṇa three times after meditation, each time believing he has found Brahman — first in food (matter), then in life-force, then in mind, then in consciousness — only to be sent back to meditate again. The final answer, which the text withholds, is ānanda: bliss. Not pleasure, but the unconditional okayness that underlies existence itself. Brahman is not a God who speaks from a burning bush. It is the hum that was already there before any bush, any voice, any ear.

This module builds the philosophical architecture of Brahman — as sat-cit-ānanda (being-consciousness-bliss), as the ineffable ground that cannot be made into an object, as the target of the famous Neti Neti ("not this, not this") negation. We contrast it with the Abrahamic personal God and Spinoza's deus sive natura, and we examine why modern physicists like Carlo Rovelli and Julian Barbour keep accidentally writing Upanishadic cosmology.

↳ Contemporary resonance: Sean Carroll's "poetic naturalism," Max Tegmark's Mathematical Universe Hypothesis, and the debate in quantum gravity over whether spacetime is fundamental or emergent — all echo the Brahman-as-ground thesis. Ecological philosopher Andreas Weber's "Enlivenment" invokes a biological version of sat-cit-ānanda.

Ātman — The Nature of the Self

6 lessons · 86 min

Yājñavalkya's wife Maitreyī asks him a question that would stop any philosopher cold: "If the whole earth, full of wealth, were mine, would I be immortal through it?" He says: no. She says: then tell me what will make me immortal. What follows, the teaching of Ātman, is one of the most extraordinary dialogues in human intellectual history — a husband dismantling every object his wife might mistake for a self: the beloved, the children, the gods, the cosmos, until only the witnessing awareness remains, which cannot be witnessed from outside itself.

Ātman is not the ego, not the personality, not the individual soul in the Christian sense. It is pure witnessing awareness — the "seer that cannot be seen, the hearer that cannot be heard." This module maps the five sheaths (pañcakośa), the nature of the witness, and the famous paradox: Ātman = Brahman. The individual awareness and the ground of all being are, upon investigation, the same thing.

↳ Contemporary resonance: David Chalmers' "hard problem of consciousness," Thomas Nagel's "What is it like to be a bat?", and Annaka Harris's Conscious all circle the Ātman question. AI consciousness debates — can GPT-4 or Claude have an "inner witness"? — make this the most practically urgent module in the curriculum.

The Four Mahāvākyas — Great Sayings

5 lessons · 70 min

Four sentences. The entire Vedanta can be distilled into four sentences, one from each of the four Vedas, each functioning less like a proposition and more like a koan — a statement so dense it requires a lifetime of return visits. Prajñānam Brahma (Consciousness is Brahman). Aham Brahmāsmi (I am Brahman). Tat tvam asi (That thou art). Ayam Ātmā Brahma (This Self is Brahman). They say the same thing from four angles, like four climbers approaching a summit from different ridges.

Tat tvam asi — "That thou art" — is perhaps the most philosophically explosive sentence in world literature. When Uddālaka tells his son Śvetaketu "you are that," he is saying: the same source from which the universe arises is the same source from which you arise. You are not a fragment of the whole. You are the whole, appearing as a fragment. Schopenhauer called this the "key to all wisdom."

↳ Contemporary resonance: Wittgenstein's limits of language, Derrida's critique of presence, and the way large language models force us to ask whether meaning lives in words or in something prior to them — all orbit the Mahāvākyas' claim that the deepest truth cannot be stated but only indicated. Also: Barack Obama's "We are the ones we've been waiting for" as an unconscious echo of Aham Brahmāsmi.

The Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upanishad

7 lessons · 105 min

The largest and arguably the oldest of the Upanishads, the Bṛhadāraṇyaka ("Great Forest Text") is also the strangest — part liturgical manual, part royal court drama, part philosophical thriller. The philosopher Yājñavalkya dominates it like a comet, speaking in koans, winning debates by terrifying the questioner into silence, and then, in a scene of remarkable intimacy, teaching his wife Maitreyī the nature of the self before walking into the forest alone.

But we also meet Gārgī Vācaknavī — a female philosopher who challenges Yājñavalkya twice in the royal debate hall, pushing him past cosmology into the ground of being itself. When he finally tells her she will "lose her head" if she asks one more question, you feel the electric charge of a mind meeting its limit. Gārgī is one of the earliest named women intellectuals in any literary tradition. Her presence here — questioning, fearless, precise — is a standing challenge to any narrative that makes Indian philosophy monolithically patriarchal.

↳ Contemporary resonance: The Bṛhadāraṇyaka's dream analysis (dreams as a middle world between waking and deep sleep) anticipates Freud and is now being revisited in light of Anil Seth's predictive processing theory of consciousness. Gārgī's sidelining after the debate mirrors contemporary #MeToo dynamics in academic philosophy departments.

The Chāndogya Upanishad

6 lessons · 92 min

The Chāndogya begins with Om — not as a religious symbol but as a philosophical problem. What is the primordial sound? Not a sound made by a mouth, but the sound that makes mouths possible. From this sonic cosmology, the text unfolds through a dazzling series of stories: Satyakāma, a boy of unknown parentage who is accepted into the gurukula on the basis of his honesty alone (a subtle blow against caste purity); Uddālaka teaching Śvetaketu the unity of all existence through the example of salt dissolved in water; the story of the young Brahmin who is sent away by his father, learns everything about the external world, and returns smug — only to be told he does not know the one thing worth knowing.

The Chāndogya also contains the Pañcāgni Vidyā — the "Five-Fire Doctrine" — which maps the journey of the soul through five cosmic fires (sky, rain, earth, man, woman), a teaching about the cycle of existence that is both ecological and eschatological. The cosmos is not a machine but a metabolism.

↳ Contemporary resonance: Satyakāma's story is the ancient India version of debates around affirmative action and "merit" (cf. Michael Sandel's The Tyranny of Merit, 2020). The Five-Fire Doctrine resonates with systems ecology and the Gaia hypothesis. Rupert Sheldrake's morphic resonance is an unwitting version of the Chāndogya's field theory of consciousness.

The Kaṭha Upanishad — Death as Teacher

5 lessons · 78 min

A young boy named Naciketas is sent — half accidentally, half in anger — to the house of Yama, the god of death. Death is away for three days. When he returns and finds a Brahmin boy waiting, he is alarmed — a guest left unhoused is a fire that burns the householder. He offers Naciketas three boons. The boy's first two boons are practical. His third is the secret of what lies beyond death. Yama tries everything to dissuade him: piles of wealth, kingdoms, the most beautiful women in the three worlds. Naciketas refuses each in turn. "All these things perish. What does not?"

What Yama finally teaches is the doctrine of the Ātman as aṇor aṇīyān mahato mahīyān — "smaller than the smallest, larger than the largest" — the famous image of the self as the rider of the chariot of the body, with intellect as charioteer, mind as reins, and the senses as horses. This chariot allegory will echo in Plato's Phaedrus, the Bhagavad Gita's battlefield, and — startlingly — in the neural architecture metaphors of contemporary cognitive science.

↳ Contemporary resonance: Ernest Becker's Denial of Death, Terror Management Theory (Greenberg, Solomon, Pyszczynski), and the modern death-awareness movement (The Death Positive Movement, Caitlin Doughty) are all incomplete approaches to the question Naciketas had the audacity to ask directly. Also: palliative care ethics, MAID (Medical Assistance in Dying) debates.

Consciousness · Sleep · Non-duality

Māṇḍūkya Upanishad — Four States of Consciousness

5 lessons · 80 min

The shortest of the major Upanishads — twelve verses — and according to Gauḍapāda (the teacher of Śaṅkarācārya's teacher), sufficient alone for liberation. The Māṇḍūkya maps four states of consciousness through the syllable AUM: waking (A), dreaming (U), deep sleep (M), and the fourth — Turīya — which is not a state at all but the witnessing awareness that underlies and transcends the other three, like the screen that remains constant while waking, dream, and dreamless sleep play out upon it.

Gauḍapāda's commentary, the Māṇḍūkya Kārikā, takes this further into ajātivāda — the doctrine of non-origination: nothing has ever truly come into existence. The universe as we know it is like a dream. This is not nihilism — it is the most radical non-dualism ever formulated. Śaṅkara would build his Advaita Vedānta on this, and modern philosophers from Schopenhauer to Timothy Sprigge to Bernardo Kastrup keep rediscovering it from the inside.

↳ Contemporary resonance: The neuroscience of sleep stages (NREM, REM) and the emerging research on "default mode network" as possibly the neural substrate of self — Judson Brewer's work on self-referential processing, and Andrew Huberman's sleep protocols — all lead back to the Māṇḍūkya's question: what exactly is consciousness doing when the ego is offline?

Karma, Rebirth & Liberation

6 lessons · 88 min

Karma is not punishment. It is not destiny. It is the most rigorous causal law in any philosophical tradition: every action (including mental action) produces a tendency (saṃskāra) that shapes future experience. No divine forgiveness can cancel it. No social status exempts you from it. And yet — here is the twist — the very awareness that witnesses karma is beyond karma. The Ātman, pure witnessing consciousness, does not accrue karma, any more than a mirror accrues the images that pass through it.

Rebirth (saṃsāra) in the Upanishads is not a consoling doctrine about reunion in heaven. It is a grim analysis of the human condition: we keep being reborn because we keep confusing the ego for the self, keep grasping at objects for the peace that only pure being provides. Liberation (mokṣa) is not escape to somewhere else. It is the recognition — right here, right now — that you were never bound. Videhamukti (liberation at death) and jīvanmukti (liberation while alive) are both possible. The latter is the richer teaching.

↳ Contemporary resonance: The neuroscience of habit formation (Charles Duhigg's The Power of Habit) is a secular karma theory. Climate change ethics requires karmic thinking across generations. Thomas Piketty's intergenerational wealth analysis is an economic saṃsāra. And the reparations debate — who bears responsibility for ancestral actions — is karma theory applied to nations.

Hermeneutics · Comparative Philosophy · Schools

Vedānta Schools — Interpreting the Upanishads

5 lessons · 75 min

The same texts. Radically different conclusions. Śaṅkara (8th c. CE), the most formidable intellect in Indian philosophy, reads the Upanishads and concludes: Advaita — there is only one, undivided, non-dual reality. Brahman alone is real. The world is māyā — not illusion exactly, but appearance, like a rope mistaken for a snake in dim light. Three centuries later, Rāmānuja reads the same texts and says: no, the world and individual souls are real — they are the body of Brahman. This is Viśiṣṭādvaita, qualified non-dualism. Then Madhva says: God, souls, and world are eternally distinct. Dvaita — pure dualism.

This is not merely academic. These three interpretations generate entirely different ethics, different paths of practice, and different answers to the question "why does suffering exist?" They also correspond, with uncanny precision, to the three major positions in contemporary philosophy of mind: eliminative materialism, property dualism, and substance dualism.

↳ Contemporary resonance: The Advaita-Dvaita debate maps onto the realism vs anti-realism debate in physics (is the wave-function real?). Rāmānuja's theology resembles process philosophy (Whitehead, Hartshorne) and has been invoked in ecotheology and deep ecology. Madhva's strict dualism resonates with the theistic traditions that dominate global religion.

Upanishads in the Modern World

4 lessons · 60 min

In 1893, a young monk from Bengal walked onto a stage at the Parliament of World Religions in Chicago, addressed the audience as "Sisters and brothers of America," and was met with a two-minute standing ovation before he had said another word. Swami Vivekananda — trained in the Advaita Vedanta of Ramakrishna — would spend the next decade planting Upanishadic ideas in the soil of Western intellectual culture. Thoreau had already been there. Emerson. Later, Aldous Huxley's Perennial Philosophy. J. Robert Oppenheimer, watching the Trinity test, whispered a line from the Bhagavad Gita: "Now I am become Death, the destroyer of worlds." The Upanishads have been present at the birth of modernity, at its horrors, and at its most searching self-questionings.

But the encounter has not been innocent. Orientalism (Said), romanticization, and the extraction of "wisdom" divorced from its social context — including the caste system the Upanishads were partially used to justify — require honest reckoning. B.R. Ambedkar, the Dalit leader and author of the Indian Constitution, rejected the Vedas and Upanishads precisely for their complicity in Brahminical supremacy. Any contemporary engagement with these texts must hold both possibilities: liberation and oppression, simultaneously real.

↳ Contemporary resonance: Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction (Jon Kabat-Zinn) as a secular Upanishadic practice. Sam Harris's Waking Up. Yuval Noah Harari's Buddhism-inflected techno-pessimism. Decolonial critiques (Frantz Fanon, Audre Lorde) applied to spiritual traditions. The commodification of yoga and meditation in wellness capitalism.

Practice · Integration · Living Philosophy

Integration & Continuing Practice

4 lessons · 55 min

The Upanishads do not end. They loop. Yājñavalkya's teaching to Maitreyī does not conclude; it opens outward into everything — into every act of noticing that the one who is noticing is not itself noticeable, into every moment of recognizing that the hunger for permanence is seeking what is already the case. The curriculum closes not with a summary but with a practice architecture: how to carry these ideas into daily life, how to create the conditions for genuine inquiry, and how to stay honest about the difference between intellectual understanding (which the Sanskrit tradition calls parokṣa jñāna) and direct recognition (aparokṣānubhūti).

The Mundaka Upanishad distinguishes between parā vidyā (higher knowledge — the knowledge of Brahman) and aparā vidyā (lower knowledge — all the sciences, arts, and conventional learning). The point is not that the sciences are worthless. It is that they do not answer the question that matters most: who is the one doing the learning? That question — alive in you, right now — is the beginning and the end of the inquiry.

↳ Contemporary resonance: Contemplative science (Richard Davidson, Clifford Saron), the emerging field of "second-person neuroscience," psychedelic-assisted therapy's non-dual states (Robin Carhart-Harris's REBUS model), and the growing movement toward contemplative education in universities (Brown's Contemplative Studies, Naropa University) — all represent institutionalized forms of this final integration.

Consciousness Science

David Chalmers' "hard problem," Integrated Information Theory (Tononi), Global Workspace Theory (Baars/Dehaene), and Anil Seth's "controlled hallucination" model of perception all approach what the Upanishads called the witness from the outside. The inside view may be the missing variable.

↳ Modules 3, 8, 11

AI & Machine Minds

If Ātman is pure witnessing awareness — the "that by which everything is known, which cannot itself be known as an object" — can a large language model be an Ātman? The Upanishadic answer is more nuanced than either Silicon Valley optimism or dismissive materialism. This is now the most pressing philosophical question of our era.

↳ Modules 3, 4, 11

Climate Ethics

The Upanishadic claim that all of existence shares a single consciousness ground — sarvam khalv idam brahma, "all this indeed is Brahman" — provides an ancient philosophical foundation for ecological ethics that runs deeper than utilitarian calculation or rights-based frameworks. Kate Raworth's Doughnut Economics and Robin Wall Kimmerer's braiding of indigenous and botanical knowledge are secular expressions of the same intuition.

↳ Modules 2, 6, 9

Identity & Social Justice

Ambedkar's critique, intersectionality theory (Kimberlé Crenshaw), and debates around caste discrimination in the Indian diaspora force a reckoning: the same texts that point beyond identity also historically were used to police it. Any serious engagement with the Upanishads in 2024 requires holding both the liberating and the oppressive potentials of this tradition simultaneously.

↳ Modules 5, 6, 11

Physics & Cosmology

Carlo Rovelli's relational quantum mechanics, Lee Smolin's loop quantum gravity, and the "it from bit" proposals of John Archibald Wheeler all raise the possibility that information or experience — not matter — is the fundamental category of reality. The Brahman hypothesis was there first, and by a margin of three millennia.

↳ Modules 2, 8, 10

Contemplative Science & Psychedelics

Robin Carhart-Harris's REBUS model (Relaxed Beliefs Under Psychedelics) proposes that classical psychedelics — psilocybin, DMT, LSD — temporarily dissolve the predictive processing hierarchy of the self, producing ego dissolution and sometimes non-dual states. This is Turīya induced pharmacologically, and the data is making hard-nosed neuroscientists sound increasingly like the Māṇḍūkya Upanishad.
