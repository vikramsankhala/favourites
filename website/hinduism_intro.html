Chapter I

What Is Hinduism?

Hinduism — known to its practitioners as Sanātana Dharma, the "Eternal Law" or "Eternal Way" — is the world's oldest living religious tradition and its most philosophically diverse. It is not a single, monolithic faith with a single founder, creed, or central institution, but rather a vast and organic constellation of beliefs, practices, philosophies, and ways of life that have evolved over more than four millennia on the Indian subcontinent.

To enter Hinduism is to enter a world of extraordinary richness: a tradition that simultaneously affirms absolute non-dualism and ecstatic devotion to personal deities; that contains rigorous metaphysical argumentation alongside ritual, myth, and mystical poetry; that has generated the world's earliest known literature and continues to evolve in the contemporary moment.

At its intellectual core lies a set of sacred texts — the Vedas and the Upanishads — and a central metaphysical inquiry into the nature of ultimate reality, which the tradition calls Brahman. These three pillars form the foundation of what Hindu philosophy calls the Jñāna Kāṇḍa: the path of knowledge.

Timeline

A Historical Overview

c. 3000–1500 BCE
Indus Valley Civilization
Urban cultures at Mohenjo-daro and Harappa show evidence of ritual bathing, proto-Śiva iconography, and fire altars — possible precursors to later Vedic and Hindu practice.
c. 1500–1200 BCE
Composition of the Rigveda
The earliest hymns of the Rigveda are composed in Vedic Sanskrit. These 1,028 hymns to cosmic deities form the bedrock of the entire Hindu scriptural tradition.
c. 1200–900 BCE
Later Vedas & Brāhmaṇas
The Samaveda, Yajurveda, and Atharvaveda take shape, followed by the Brāhmaṇas — complex prose texts explaining the theology of Vedic sacrifice.
c. 800–200 BCE
The Upanishadic Age
Philosophical dialogues between teachers and students produce the Upanishads — meditations on Ātman (the inner self) and Brahman (ultimate reality) that transform Vedic religion into a profound metaphysical system.
c. 500 BCE – 500 CE
Classical Hindu Synthesis
The two great epics — the Mahābhārata (containing the Bhagavad Gītā) and the Rāmāyaṇa — are composed. The six classical schools of philosophy (darśanas) crystallize. Devotional religion (bhakti) begins to flourish.
788–820 CE
Ādi Śaṅkarācārya
The great philosopher-monk Śaṅkara systematizes Advaita Vedānta — the doctrine of non-dual Brahman — and writes commentaries on the Upanishads, the Brahma Sūtras, and the Bhagavad Gītā.
Modern Era
Global Hinduism
Reformers like Ramakrishna, Swami Vivekananda, and Sri Aurobindo reinterpret Hindu thought for modern contexts. Hinduism becomes a global presence while remaining rooted in the ancient Vedic-Upanishadic tradition.
Chapter II

The Vedas

Śruti — "That Which Was Heard"

The word Veda derives from the Sanskrit root vid — "to know." The Vedas are thus, literally, the Books of Knowledge. But in the Hindu understanding, they are far more than books: they are śruti, meaning "that which was heard" — eternal, uncreated sound vibrations that were perceived in deep meditation by ancient seers (ṛṣis) and transmitted orally from teacher to student across hundreds of generations before they were ever written down.

There are four Vedas, each with four distinct layers of text. Together they form the most comprehensive religious literature of the ancient world — a corpus spanning cosmic hymns, ritual manuals, mystical forest treatises, and the philosophical dialogues of the Upanishads.

The Vedas are the breath of the Supreme Being. As a man breathes, so the Vedas are breathed out by Brahman. One must study the Vedas.
— Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad II.4.10
Veda Primary Purpose Character Associated Deity
Rigveda Collection of 1,028 hymns (sūktas) to the gods Oldest; lyrical, cosmological poetry. Includes the Purusha Sūkta and the Nāsadīya Sūkta (hymn of creation) Agni, Indra, Soma, Varuna
Sāmaveda Liturgical melodies for Soma sacrifice Largely drawn from the Rigveda but set to musical notation; the "Veda of Song." Foundation of Indian classical music Soma
Yajurveda Prose formulas (yajus) for ritual priests Practical ritual manual; exists in "Black" (Kṛṣṇa) and "White" (Śukla) recensions. Contains the Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa Yajña (sacrifice itself)
Atharvaveda Spells, charms, healing hymns, cosmological speculation Closer to folk religion; includes hymns on kingship, cosmology, and the nature of time (Kāla). Contains early precursors to Upanishadic thought Atharvan seers; Brahman

The Four Layers of Each Veda

🔥
Saṃhitā
The core collection of hymns and mantras. These are the most ancient layer — lyrical invocations of cosmic powers understood both as nature deities and as aspects of a single underlying reality. The Rigveda Saṃhitā is the oldest known literary text in any Indo-European language.
🕯️
Brāhmaṇa
Prose commentaries on ritual procedure, explaining the meaning and purpose of Vedic sacrifices. The Brāhmaṇas represent a priestly elaboration of Vedic religion, systematizing the correspondence between cosmic order (ṛta) and ritual action. The Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa is among the most important.
🌿
Āraṇyaka
The "Forest Books" — composed for renunciants who had retired from household life into the forest. These transitional texts begin to shift emphasis from external ritual to internal, contemplative equivalents. They form a bridge between ritualism and philosophy.
✨
Upaniṣad
The philosophical summit of the Vedic corpus. Sometimes called the Vedānta — "End (or Fulfillment) of the Vedas" — the Upanishads turn from ritual performance to the deepest questions of existence: What is the self? What is ultimate reality? What is liberation? These are explored next in full.
The Vedic Worldview: Ṛta and Dharma
Central to the Vedas is the concept of ṛta — the cosmic order that underlies both the physical universe (the regular rising of the sun, the turning of the seasons) and the moral order (truth, right action, fidelity to one's role). Ṛta is upheld by Varuṇa, the guardian of cosmic law. From ṛta is derived the concept of dharma, which becomes the cornerstone of all subsequent Hindu ethics and cosmology. To live in accordance with dharma is to align oneself with the deepest grain of reality itself.
Chapter III

The Upanishads

Vedānta — The Fulfillment of Vedic Wisdom

The word Upaniṣad is traditionally derived from the Sanskrit roots upa (near), ni (down), and sad (to sit) — meaning "to sit down near." It evokes the image of a student sitting at the feet of a teacher, receiving secret wisdom transmitted in intimate dialogue. This image captures something essential about the Upanishads: they are not systematic treatises but living conversations — exchanges between seekers and sages about the deepest mysteries of existence.

There are over 200 texts that bear the name Upaniṣad, but the 13 principal Upanishads (mukhya upaniṣads) form the canonical core, quoted and commented upon by all the major schools of Vedānta philosophy. The oldest date to approximately 800 BCE; others were composed centuries later.

Tat tvam asi — Thou art That. The deepest Self within you is identical with the ultimate ground of all being.
— Chāndogya Upaniṣad VI.8.7 (one of the "Great Sayings," Mahāvākyas)

The 13 Principal Upanishads

Upanishad Associated Veda Key Teachings
Bṛhadāraṇyaka Śukla Yajurveda Longest and oldest; contains the great dialogue of Yājñavalkya. Teaches the identity of Ātman and Brahman, and the doctrine of rebirth and karma.
Chāndogya Sāmaveda Contains tat tvam asi. Teaches Brahman as the subtle inner essence of all things (sarvam khalv idam brahma — "All of this is indeed Brahman").
Taittirīya Kṛṣṇa Yajurveda The doctrine of the five sheaths (pañca kośa) of the self. Brahman as ānanda (bliss) — "He who knows Brahman as bliss is not afraid."
Aitareya Rigveda Consciousness (prajñā) as Brahman. The cosmogonic story of Ātman creating the universe from itself.
Kaṭha Kṛṣṇa Yajurveda The boy Naciketa and Yama (Death) — teaching on the eternal Ātman. Contains the famous chariot metaphor for the self, senses, and intellect.
Īśā Śukla Yajurveda Shortest principal Upanishad (18 verses). Teaches that the Lord (Īśa) pervades all — one must act without attachment, renouncing ownership.
Kena Sāmaveda "By whom is the mind directed?" — inquiry into the unknown knower behind all knowing. Brahman as that which cannot be an object of knowledge.
Praśna Atharvaveda Six questions about creation, prāṇa (life-force), sleep, consciousness, Oṃkāra, and the nature of the sixteen-fold person (ṣoḍaśakala puruṣa).
Muṇḍaka Atharvaveda Distinction between higher knowledge (Brahman) and lower knowledge (the Vedas, sciences). The metaphor of Brahman as a bow and the self as an arrow.
Māṇḍūkya Atharvaveda Only 12 verses, yet considered the most important. Analysis of the four states of consciousness (waking, dreaming, deep sleep, turīya) and Oṃ as the symbol of Brahman.
Śvetāśvatara Kṛṣṇa Yajurveda Theistic; identifies Brahman with Rudra/Śiva. One of the earliest systematic theologies in Indian thought.
Aitareya Rigveda Prajñānam brahma — "Consciousness is Brahman." One of the four Mahāvākyas.
Maitrī (Maitrāyaṇī) Kṛṣṇa Yajurveda Later Upanishad synthesizing earlier thought; introduces the concept of the three guṇas (qualities) of nature that become central to Sāṃkhya philosophy.

The Four Great Sayings (Mahāvākyas)

Each of the four Vedas contributes one of the principal statements of Upanishadic metaphysics.

Prajñānam Brahma
Consciousness is Brahman
From the Aitareya Upanishad (Rigveda). Consciousness is not a product of matter or the body — it is the fundamental nature of ultimate reality itself. To know is to be Brahman.
Aham Brahmāsmi
I am Brahman
From the Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upanishad (Yajurveda). The deepest "I" — not the ego, but the pure witnessing awareness — is none other than Brahman, infinite and eternal.
Tat Tvam Asi
Thou Art That
From the Chāndogya Upanishad (Sāmaveda). Repeated nine times by the sage Uddālaka to his son Śvetaketu — the individual self (Ātman) is identical to the universal ground (Brahman). There is no ultimate separation.
Ayam Ātmā Brahma
This Self is Brahman
From the Māṇḍūkya Upanishad (Atharvaveda). The Ātman — the innermost self behind all states of consciousness — is Brahman, the infinite, unconditioned ground of all existence.
The Method of the Upanishads
The Upanishads do not proceed by assertion alone, but by a method of negation (neti, neti — "not this, not this") combined with direct pointing. When Yājñavalkya is asked "What is Ātman?", he famously replies: "It is not this, not this" — the self cannot be reduced to any object, because it is the very subject of all experience. Simultaneously, the sages point to immediate experience: "That by which you see, hear, think, and know — that is Brahman; everything else is miserable." This double movement — via negativa and direct recognition — forms the heart of Upanishadic teaching.
Chapter IV

Brahman

The Ground of All Being

The word Brahman (not to be confused with Brahmin, the priestly caste, or Brahmā, the creator deity) derives from the Sanskrit root bṛh — "to grow, swell, expand." It points to that which is infinitely vast, boundless, the inexhaustible ground from which all existence arises and to which it returns.

Brahman is the central metaphysical concept of the Upanishads and the foundational principle of all Vedānta philosophy. It is defined by three primary attributes: Sat (pure being, existence), Cit (pure consciousness, awareness), and Ānanda (pure bliss). Together these form the formula Saccidānanda — the most complete description the tradition offers of ultimate reality.

Yet even this description is understood to fall short. Brahman is ultimately nirguṇa — without qualities, attributes, or limitations. Any positive description is a pointer, not an adequate characterization. As the Taittirīya Upaniṣad says: "From which words turn back, unable to reach, along with the mind — one who knows the bliss of Brahman is not afraid."

Pure Being
Sat — सत्
Brahman simply is — it is existence itself, not a being among beings. It cannot come into being or cease to be, for it is the very ground of all being. Perishable things exist within Brahman; they do not exist as fully as Brahman.
Pure Consciousness
Cit — चित्
Brahman is not unconscious matter — it is luminous awareness. Consciousness is not an emergent property of the physical world but its ground. The universe does not produce consciousness; consciousness is the medium in which the universe appears.
Pure Bliss
Ānanda — आनन्द
Brahman is ānanda — not happiness dependent on circumstances, but uncaused, unconditional bliss. The Taittirīya Upanishad contains a remarkable meditation on the "bliss-body" (ānandamaya kośa), suggesting that the deepest level of our being is bliss identical with Brahman.

Saguna vs. Nirguna Brahman

Nirguṇa Brahman — Without Attributes

The ultimate, absolute Brahman — infinite, formless, beyond all qualities and description. This is Brahman as it is in itself, prior to all conceptualization.

"Neti, neti" — not this, not this. All concepts ultimately fail. Brahman exceeds every definition, every category, every limitation.

This aspect of Brahman is the basis for the Advaita Vedānta of Śaṅkara — the school of pure non-dualism, in which the apparent multiplicity of the world is ultimately māyā (appearance, illusion), and only Brahman is real.

Saguṇa Brahman — With Attributes

Brahman as approached through qualities: as omniscient, omnipotent, all-loving; as the personal God (Īśvara) who creates, sustains, and dissolves the universe. Known through devotion, ritual, and relationship.

This is the basis for Viśiṣṭādvaita (Rāmānuja) — "qualified non-dualism" — in which souls and the world are real but exist as the body of Brahman/God; and for Dvaita (Madhva) — dualism — in which God and individual souls are eternally distinct.

The great devotional traditions of Hinduism — worship of Viṣṇu/Kṛṣṇa, Śiva, Devī — are all oriented toward Saguṇa Brahman.

Brahman is the Truth, Knowledge, and Infinity. He who knows Brahman hidden in the cave of the heart in the highest space, he enjoys all desires, together with the omniscient Brahman.
— Taittirīya Upaniṣad II.1

Ātman: The Individual Self

The Upanishads teach that the individual self — Ātman — is ultimately identical with Brahman. The apparent separateness of individual souls is a product of avidyā (ignorance) and māyā (the power of appearance/illusion). Beneath the multiplicity of names and forms, there is one undivided reality.

The Kaṭha Upaniṣad offers the famous chariot metaphor: the body is the chariot, the senses are the horses, the mind is the reins, the intellect (buddhi) is the charioteer, and the Ātman is the rider — the pure witness who is never destroyed when the chariot is dismantled.

The Taittirīya Upanishad presents a layered model of the human self — five concentric "sheaths" (kośas) surrounding the innermost Ātman: (1) Annamaya kośa — the physical body, made of food; (2) Prāṇamaya kośa — the vital/breath body, the life-force animating the physical; (3) Manomaya kośa — the mental body, the seat of emotion and thought; (4) Vijñānamaya kośa — the intellect-body, the discriminating wisdom-mind; (5) Ānandamaya kośa — the bliss-body, experienced in deep dreamless sleep. Beyond all five sheaths is the pure Ātman — Brahman itself — which is not a sheath at all, but the luminous awareness in which all sheaths appear.
The Māṇḍūkya Upanishad — one of the most compact and profound texts in world philosophy — analyzes consciousness through three ordinary states and a fourth: Jāgrat (waking consciousness), where the self experiences the outer world through the senses; Svapna (dream state), where the mind creates its own world of appearances; Suṣupti (deep dreamless sleep), where all experience ceases and there is undifferentiated bliss; and Turīya ("the fourth") — not a fourth state added to the three, but the silent, aware ground in which all three states arise and subside. Turīya is Brahman — pure, unconditioned awareness — recognized as one's own deepest nature.
If Brahman alone is real, why does the multiplicity of the world appear? The tradition's answer is māyā — a concept far subtler than simple "illusion." Māyā is Brahman's own creative power of self-concealment and manifestation. It is not "unreal" (the world genuinely appears and functions) but it is not ultimately real either (it has no existence independent of Brahman). The classic analogy: a rope mistaken for a snake in twilight. The "snake" is not real — but the fear it causes is real, and only when the light reveals the rope does the snake vanish. Similarly, ignorance (avidyā) causes us to mistake the world of multiplicity for ultimate reality; wisdom (jñāna) reveals that Brahman alone is.
The Upanishads introduce and develop the doctrine of karma (action and its consequences) and saṃsāra (the cycle of birth, death, and rebirth). The Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upanishad first clearly articulates: "According as one acts, according as one conducts oneself, so does one become." Each action leaves a trace (saṃskāra) that shapes the character of the self and determines the conditions of future lives. Liberation (mokṣa) consists in the complete dissolution of the false identification of Ātman with the body-mind complex — the recognition that one is Brahman, which was never born and can never die. For such a one, the cycle of rebirth ends.
Chapter V

Key Concepts

The Vocabulary of Hindu Philosophy

Dharma — धर्म
Cosmic & Moral Order
Often translated as "duty," "righteousness," or "law," dharma is far richer: it is the inherent nature and ordering principle of all things. Cosmic dharma holds the universe together; social dharma prescribes one's duties according to role and stage of life; personal dharma is the unique calling of each individual soul.
Karma — कर्म
Action & Consequence
Every intentional action leaves a trace in consciousness that shapes future experience and rebirth. Karma is not fate but moral physics: the universe tends toward equilibrium. The Bhagavad Gītā offers a revolutionary teaching: act according to dharma, without attachment to results, and karma does not bind.
Mokṣa — मोक्ष
Liberation
The ultimate goal of human life in the Hindu framework: liberation from the cycle of birth and death (saṃsāra) through the realization of one's identity with Brahman. Also called mukti. Different schools envision mokṣa differently — as merger with Brahman, as eternal companionship with God, or as the cessation of suffering.
Avidyā — अविद्या
Ignorance
The root cause of suffering (duḥkha) and bondage is not sin but ignorance — the mistaken identification of the self with the body, mind, and ego rather than with the infinite Ātman-Brahman. This is not intellectual ignorance but a deeper existential misidentification that meditation, inquiry, and grace work to dissolve.
Ṛta & Satya — ऋत & सत्य
Cosmic Truth
Ṛta is the Vedic concept of cosmic order — the invisible law that ensures the sun rises, seasons turn, and right action bears fruit. Satya (truth) is its ethical expression — truthfulness is considered the supreme virtue in most Hindu ethical traditions. "Satyam eva jayate" — Truth alone triumphs.
Puruṣārthas — पुरुषार्थ
The Four Goals of Life
Hindu ethics recognizes four legitimate human aims: Dharma (righteousness), Artha (prosperity, material welfare), Kāma (pleasure, love, beauty), and Mokṣa (liberation). All four are honored; none is to be pursued at the expense of the others. Mokṣa is the highest, but the others are real goods.

The Three Paths to Liberation

Path (Mārga/Yoga) Meaning Method Key Text
Jñāna Yoga Path of Knowledge Philosophical inquiry, meditation, and direct recognition of Ātman as Brahman. Discrimination (viveka) between the real and unreal; dispassion (vairāgya); self-surrender. Culminates in the direct experience of non-duality. Upanishads, Advaita Vedānta
Bhakti Yoga Path of Devotion Loving surrender to a personal form of God (Kṛṣṇa, Rāma, Devī, Śiva). Through worship, prayer, chanting (kīrtana), and the cultivation of divine love, the devotee's heart is purified and the ego dissolved in God's grace. Bhagavad Gītā, Bhāgavata Purāṇa
Karma Yoga Path of Action Selfless action performed as an offering to God or as service to all beings, without attachment to results. The secret lies not in renouncing action but in renouncing the ego that acts — acting from one's deepest nature, not from craving or aversion. Bhagavad Gītā II–III
Chapter VI

Schools of Vedānta

How Philosophers Have Understood Brahman

The Upanishads do not speak with a single voice, and the tradition has generated extraordinary philosophical debate about their meaning. Three schools of Vedānta — all accepting the Upanishads, Brahma Sūtras, and Bhagavad Gītā as authoritative — have produced the most influential interpretations.

Śaṅkara's Advaita (lit. "not two") is the most rigorous non-dualistic philosophy ever formulated. Its central thesis: Brahman alone is real; the world of multiplicity is appearance (vivartavāda); and the individual self (jīva) is ultimately identical to Brahman. The experience of being a separate self is produced by māyā — Brahman's inexplicable power of self-concealment. Liberation is not the attainment of Brahman but the recognition that one has always already been Brahman. Śaṅkara developed the concept of two levels of truth: at the empirical level, the world and individual selves are real; at the absolute level, only Brahman exists. His commentaries on the ten principal Upanishads, the Brahma Sūtras, and the Bhagavad Gītā remain unsurpassed in their philosophical depth.
Rāmānuja's Viśiṣṭādvaita (lit. "non-dualism with distinctions") accepts Brahman as the only ultimate reality, but insists that individual souls (cit) and the material world (acit) are real — not illusions — and exist as the "body" of Brahman/God (Viṣṇu/Nārāyaṇa). The relationship is one of inseparable difference-in-unity: souls are genuinely distinct from God, yet have no existence independent of him. Liberation is not absorption into Brahman but eternal loving communion with God in his highest realm (Vaikuṇṭha). Rāmānuja's theology forms the philosophical foundation of the great devotional Vaiṣṇava traditions (including the later traditions of Caitanya and the Haridāsī saints).
Madhva's Dvaita (lit. "two") insists on five eternal distinctions: between God (Viṣṇu) and individual souls; between God and matter; between individual souls themselves; between souls and matter; and between different forms of matter. God alone is independent (svatantra); everything else is eternally dependent (paratantra) on him. Liberation is the eternal, distinct enjoyment of God's bliss in his presence — not merger, not even the bliss-body identity of Advaita. Madhva's sharp dualism provides a robust theological basis for theistic devotion and personal relationship with God.
The great Bengali saint Caitanya Mahāprabhu taught an achintya (inconceivable) relationship between Brahman/Kṛṣṇa and individual souls — simultaneously different and non-different, in a way that exceeds human logic. This position preserves both the devotional possibility of real loving relationship with God and the non-dual insight that God is the all. Caitanya's tradition became one of the most vibrant devotional movements in Indian history, and in the 20th century reached global audiences through the work of A.C. Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupāda (ISKCON).
Beyond Schools: The Living Tradition
While these philosophical systems represent Hinduism's intellectual pinnacle, it is important to remember that Hinduism is above all a lived tradition. For hundreds of millions of people, it is the daily act of lighting a lamp before the household shrine, the sound of the conch shell at dawn, the smell of jasmine and sandalwood in a temple, the songs of the bhakti saints, the observance of the seasons through festival and ritual, and the quiet turning inward in meditation. The philosophy and the practice, the thought and the devotion, the cosmic and the intimate — in Hinduism, all these are held together in a single, inexhaustible embrace.
